Laos

Laos has built the brand new Lao-China Railway, conencting the major cities. This is a huge advantage to travel quickly and nicely. The local busses in the smaller villages are very cheap and accesible. Overall, Lao transport is solid, but nothing flashy Thailand transport score: 7.1/10

Currency Conversion: 10 USD ≈ 200,000 LAK (Lao Kip)

Main languages: Lao

Thailand

Since Thailand is a tourist playground, there is robust transportation infrastructure. You can either book transportation through 12Go App or any hotel/hostel. Transportation ranges from high-speed ferries to islands to 10-person shuttles. The public transport is not widely used; it is mostly tourist-specific transportation infrastructure. Thailand transport score: 8.2/10

Currency Conversion: 10 USD ≈ 345 THB (Thai Baht)

Main languages: Thai, English, Russian

Nepal

Kathmandu is a small city with tight streets. There are often traffic jams due to motorbikes and cars whizzing around. There are not really any street lights or street signs. The only way to get around is via taxi, which you have to hail. There is a ride-share app but I have not tried it. Nepal transport score: 4.9/10

Currency Conversion: 10 USD ≈ 1350 NPR (Nepalese Rupee)

Main languages: Nepali, Chinese, Hindi, English

UAE

Dubai is easy to get around. You can hop in a taxi or Uber anywhere. There is also a metro system that requires a distinct metro card, but it takes a long time. UAE transport score: 6.9/10

Currency Conversion: 10 USD ≈ 37 AED (UAE Dirham)

Main languages: Arabic, English, Russian, Chinese

Rwanda

In Kigali, there are thousands of motorcycle taxis. You can get one wherever on the side of the street. A ride for ten minutes costs 75 cents. There is a bus system but it is not extensive. The city is layered with hills so getting around on the motorcycles is quick, easy, and efficient. Rwanda transport score: 6.7/10

Currency Conversion: 10 USD ≈ 13,500 RWF (Rwandan Francs)

Main languages: Kinyarwanda, French, English, Swahili

Tanzania

The best way to get around in Tanzania is by taxi or public shuttle. Similar to Kenyan matatus, Tanzania has dala-dalas, which are small, crowded, and unpredictable in schedule. Tanzania transport score: 4.1/10

Currency Conversion: 10 USD ≈ 27,000 TZS (Tanzanian Shilling)

Main languages: Swahili, English

Kenya

Uber is the main mode of transport in Kenya. A 45-minute Uber ride can cost less than 10 USD. You can also order a motorcycle for a cheaper, quicker option. Trains connect Nairobi to Mombasa, and buses travel to Tanzania and Uganda. Matatus, privately-owned minibuses, operate on set routes but don’t follow strict schedules. Many are decorated with vibrant designs and even Israeli flags. Kenya transport score: 6.2/10

Currency Conversion: 10 USD ≈ 1,300 KES (Kenyan Shilling)

Main languages: Swahili, English

Georgia

In Tbilisi, a scenic cable car ride runs from Rike Park to the Narikala Fortress. The bus system in Tbilisi is efficient, with dedicated lanes and easy mobile payments. Buses cost 1 GEL (about 37 cents) and even have USB-C charging capabilities. Trains connect Tbilisi to Batumi, and car rentals are available for exploring smaller cities. Georgia transport score: 8.8/10

Currency Conversion: 10 USD ≈ 27 GEL (Georgian Lari)

Main languages: Georgian, Russian, English

Israel

Israel has robust public transport. Trains connect the airport to major cities, and the Jerusalem Light Rail provides local service. A Rav Kav card is required for public transit. For ride-hailing, Gett is an alternative to Uber, and the Moovit app helps with navigation. Buses are extensive but Google Maps sometimes lacks accurate timing. Israel transport score: 7.8/10

Currency Conversion: 10 USD ≈ 38 ILS (Israeli New Shekel)

Main languages: Hebrew, English, French, Russian
